Well, first of all, I'd like to clarify that all of this is my humble opinion on the few courses that I've been able to take on some MOOC platforms, namely [Udacity](https://www.udacity.com/) , [Coursera](https://www.coursera.org/) , [CodeSchool](https://www.codeschool.com/) and [CodeCademy](http://www.codecademy.com/). I'm not really an early adopter for those, as I've been engaged in a formal education degree. But I've been watching it grow on the news, and two years ago during the *'Hour of Code'* I downloaded some of the apps and started some courses.

The approach I'm taking is equal for all of them. The main criteria where: credentials and recognition, their partners, diversity of courses and the teaching system. I've given a glance for fees and pricing, but as for now I took only free courses this part might not be accurate. For each platform, I'm gonna have a little description and a pro's and con's table. If you want to know what courses I'm basing myself in, I'll keep record on the courses I have or am taking right [here](/MOOCs/)


Udacity
===
It was the first one in which I've completed a course. They have very good teacher's, and many courses are built with support of companies such as Google, Facebook and Amazon.

The teaching system often includes a step-by-step tools and framework installation, and also a very living community. The teaching system focus on methods, processes and how-to's, but also goes for the formal part when it's needed.

I was always surprised by the quality of the challenges and projects proposed, and at many points the teachers insisted for us to 'google it if you have an issue', explaining that that would be a great part of everyday routine of a programmer.

They also happen to offer 'Nanodegrees' for a few specialties, with certificates.

Pros | Cons
:-----: | :-----:
recognized by internet companies | all the courses focus technology skills\*
sponsored courses | english only
courses range from syntax to methods and more | high fees
focus on methods |
offers 'nanodegrees' |

\* *Well, this is not necessarily a con if you are looking after technology courses, as I am. But to be precise I prefer to add it.*

---

CodeCademy
===
Their main audience is a little different - people who never coded before and who are willing to learn to code interactively from their browser. Well, we can get some insight from that.

First of all: if we're learning to code from the browser that means we'll never be learning to open a real IDE or powerful text editor. Secondly, being for the complete novice, it helps them keep motivated with gamification techniques, which is a very good point.

Their focus in on language syntax, and their courses are great quickstarters to anyone who's comfortable with coding in other languages. But you might never get to algorithms if you just jump from course to course on this platform.I'm not sure if it has any industry recognition, but it doesn't showcase any.

Pros | Cons
:-----: | :-----:
very interactive and gambles hardly on gamification | won't always get to the real perks of coding
very accessible for begginers (from 20$/month) | projects are only accessible for pro users|
available in languages  | doesn't showcase any industry credentials
 | doesn't showcase any partnerships

*Good to start, but I wouldn't stick with it for too long on the same language*

---

CodeSchool
===
The first time I started a course, it was pretty much the same as CodeCademy, focusing mostly on the syntax. But over time they became way more broad on their approach, and since they started adding teacher's video and 'make it from scratch' screencasts, the quality and depth of the content has increased a lot.

I personally think that was all about being sold to Pluralsight. They also have gamification elements, but it doesn't stand out as much as in CodeCademy.

A new feature I haven't had a chance to test is the Team Account system, which seems interesting to keep people motivated.

Pros | Cons
:-----: | :-----:
Pluralsight standard | all the courses focus technology skills \*
Microsoft recognition (through Pluralsight partnership) | english only
courses range from syntax to methods and more |
offers challenges, screencasts and others |
very accessible for begginers (from 29$/month) |
offers some free courses |

\* *As I said before, this can be a good thing.*

---

Pluralsight
===
This is an official Microsoft partner.  I had a chance to get a 1 month free subscription to Pluralsight earlier this year. Even though I didn't have as much time as would have liked to test different courses, I was able to complete two AutoCad courses, which were very good.

The courses consisted of a mindful step-by-step or how-to with the teacher making each step with you on the project they proposed.
They offer many platform specific courses, being great for anyone who needs help on a new software.

Pros | Cons
:-----: | :-----:
Microsoft recognition | offers a weekly free course
wide variety of courses |
very accessible for begginers (from 29$/month) |

---

Coursera.org
===
Well, this is certainly the most well known. It also happens to have many high-rank worldwide universities, including Yale, Stanford, Princeton on the US and many others worldwide. In Brazil, partnerships include the prestigious ITA, UNICAMP and USP.

It is not only focused on technology, so if you are looking for business, arts or even language courses this one is great. Because they have so many producers, the teaching system varies - and so does quality of the courses -  but their guidelines are firm about it being dynamic and interactive.

Courses tend to be very time consuming - they tend to have a lot of activities and quizzes, so you should watch out for the time you can devote to your MOOC.

Pros | Cons
:-----: | :-----:
offers certificates | certificates are not free
it is the most recognized on the market | some courses are time consuming
many partners worldwide |  
very diversified portfolio |
as content depends on producer, there are many  languages |
the teaching system varies |
most of the content is free |

As you can see, Coursera has many pros, and I really recommend it for anyone willing to learn new on the internet.

---
Well, to summarize, there's something good in all of them. It is up to you to choose the approach you want for your learning experience.
You can choose depending on the time you have, what you are willing to spend, on which language and how diversified courses have to be.

There's no harm in going for the best of each - I tend to go first for a look at CodeCademy to check on the syntax, and if there's anything that won't work I go look for some more guidance on Udacity or CodeSchool.

Remember, those are just tools for your goal - learning something new. It is always up to you to make the best of them.
